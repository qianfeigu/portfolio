A Game Designed for
Espresso House

UX Design / Evaluations

Team project/ Feb. 2020 - Apr. 2020
The project was innitiated by the course “Evaluating Interactions” in Aalto by Associate Professor Andrés Lucero. Being a 6-week intensive course, the 1st week demanded us to conduct field observation to find out starting point, the 2nd and 3rd week required us to conduct two rounds of sprint to develop the prototype from the starting point, while during the 4-6 weeks, we evaluated our prototype with qualitative and quantitative approaches.

Project Overview

This project is a 6-week intensive project.


Week 1: Field observation to set design challenge

Week 2: Sprint round 1, generate prototype

Week 3: Sprint round 2, iterate prototype

Weeks 4-6: Evaluation plan, conduct and analysis

Concept Generation

Theoretical Framework

Introduced by the teacher of the course, F-formation is also known as facing formation (McDermott et al. 1978). It is the spatial pattern sustained during social interactions (e.g., talking, working, playing chess, eating at a table and offering a cup of water).

“An F-formation arises whenever two or more people sustain a spatial and orientational relationship in which the space between them is one to which they have equal, direct, and exclusive access.” (Kendon 1990)

Field observation

We got to know different kinds of f-formations that people would form in real-life situations. Under this framework, we went on filed observation in several highly populated places in Helsinki and Espoo, including Kamppi shopping center, Espres- so House Kamppi, Iso Omena shopping center and in Helsinki subway train. We spotted unuaual f-formations that we see as the starting points of the project.

At Kamppi shopping center, we mainly focused on different formation within a group, for example group of 1, 2 or 3. At Iso Omena shopping center we stood on a balcony that could see people flow on the first floor. On observing Espresso House Kamppi, we found the f-formation interesting when people queueing in line waiting for ordering.

  

Observing people waiting in line at the counter we found different types of groups: group of one, two or three people. Within an hour of observation, there were 11 groups of 2-people groups. The f-formation is different under various circumstances.

Design challenges 

From our field observation, we found that people’s f-formations in Espresso House are differentiated by payment method, especial- ly in those 2-people groups: 2 people paying individually or by one of them. In the former situation, who’s paying is the dominant in the conversation with barista, while the other people takes the outer position in the r-space, talks more with the other than with the barista. We also tried to observe the relationship between two people, mostly being couples, parent-child, and friends, so we could guess the scenario of paying. While in the other situation, people pay their bills individually, people tended to form circular formation where people have the same chance to talk to barista and each other.


Sprint Overview

With the help of the book Sprint: How to solve big problems and test new ideas in just five days (Knapp J, Zeratsky J, Kowitz B, 2016), we planed our two rounds of sprint. We chose 3 people for the roles: one decider, one facilirator and one interviewer.

Mapping

According to the insights we got from the field observation, with the help of two coffee-goers’ information in the expert interview, we listed out all the pain points according to the differnet stages in the queueing line, and create HMW questions accordingly.

After reclustering all the notes, we found 4 themes:
- to make the information (menu) transparent and accessible
- to improve the efficiency during queueing & paying
- to emotionally support, give more control to customers
- to encourage the spontaneous interactions among customers
Gathering the votes from all team members, we finally chose the third as our main theme, hence our design brief:

HOW MIGHT WE provide an alternative ordering experience which is less intimidating so people feel comfortable and take their time?

Sketching

On Tuesday, we gathered inspirations individually, and dicussed how to utilise the inspirations into our design brief.
At the end of the day, we created our own ideas individually within time limit.

Storyboarding

On Wednesday, each one of us showcased our sketched ideas, explained to the others and voted blindly. The ideas below on the post-its are the ones we all agreed to keep and combine.

We decided to use digital solutions with several features:
- “Coffee based on mood” Recommendation, which could help people decide and for those willing to try new drinks
- “Coffee Passport”, aka. your personal coffee profile that remem- bers your taste and special requirements, with recommendations according your previous purchase
- Coffee Game, with an interesting, adventurousexperience
- Social attributes: Birthday Coffee Sharing

Prototyping

Based on the fact that Espresso House has already launched their app, with the functions to order and pay through the app, access to Espresso House membership card (as showed on the right). We decided to keep the already existing functions, add our personalised recommendation feature into the app.

On the rest of Thursday, we made our interactive prototype.

Testing

Setting

On Friday, we reserved two rooms for evaluations. We decorated the interview room, trying to recreate the environment of Espresso House, with coffee shop background noice and the field observation video at Espresso House playing. We also made coffee and pastries, to let the participants rest and having coffee during the test.

At the same time in the other room, the other team members are watch- ing the interview remotely and taking notes on post-its.

Interview process 

1. Warm-up

We introduced the basics of our prototype and asked the participants to check and sign the concent form. Also we prepared several question related to the habit of during coffee and going to coffee shops.

2. Testing out prototype

During the test, we asked our participants to think aloud to gather the most instant and accurate feedback.

3. Post-test questionnaire & interview

At the end of the test, we gave them a questionnarie to validate the usability of the prototype. With the help of the open-ended questions, we also gathered the personal preference of the participants.

Prototype Iteration

Tackling the problems

Getting all the feedback from Sprint round 1, we should focus on:

- Media Type: Suitability, accessibility, expectation

- Novelty on Recommendation Feature

- Ordering Dynamics: customer-barista relations, queue experience

- Purpose: having a coffee, socialising, working

- Latent Needs

- Functionalities: control, information, types of Notification

The first and the most important problem is about the media type of the prototype: whether it being a mobile application, a game on the interactive screen at the entrance, or others? After searching and analysing, we decided to use an HTML webpage on a poster, so the customers can scan the QR code on the poster and access the prototype.

The other question is how to make the prototype more fun? Instead of combining the Espresso House application, we decided to mainly focus on the recommendation feature itself. So how to make it more fun, at the same time give customers decent amount of control?

- Coffee Trivia Quiz

- Interactive Video Walk-through

- Self-service Systems

- Mobile Gaming

Inspired by the games, movies and TV show, we found our way: to make the prototype a game, during which the customers could get a coffee recommendation rounds of questions based on luck, and they could also make coffee for friend and receive coffee made by him/her. In this way they can surprise each other and get recommendations.

User Scenario
Imagine you arrive to meet your friend at Espresso House. The atmosphere is lively and you join a large queue. There might be ten or twelve people waiting to place their order. Your friend asks you to “play this game” with him/her that he/she discovered from a nearby poster.

Evaluation Plan 

Research Question
Does (mobile) gaming add value to a coffee ordering experience?

Theoretical framework

According to the Practise theory, “different meanings can be attached to coffee ordering as a practise, for example convenience, novelty, benefits, and authenticity”. We acknowledge that the artefact (the game) receive meanings through related practise (coffee ordering) (Jackson & Holbrook, 1995). Bäckström (2011) stress that “one and the same consumer may ascribe multiple meanings to their leisure shopping” (p. 205).

Evaluation is based on conducting experiments, where hypotheses are tested to make a prediction about the value (see research question) that key stakeholders (see stakeholders) place on the app. Hypothesis exam- ines a relationship between selected variables (Sharp et al, 2019).

Stakeholders (Participants)

- Users as customers of Espresso House

- Staff of Espresso House (expert interview)

Breaking Down the Research Questions 

By exploring all our research questions (left below), we found our better way to evaluate key stakeholders’ experience with and without (normal) the gaming prototype. We decided to compare the date gather under these two situations.

1. What is currently valuable to the stakeholders (customers and coffee shop personnel) in this coffee ordering experience?

2. How does a game actually affect the current ordering experience?

Evaluation Plan

According to the two research questions mentioned, we planned for the evaluation with 3 phases.

During phase 1, we planned to go to Espresso House Kamppi, and gather quantitative by sending out questionnaire, and qualitative data by conduct- ing expert interview to whom we filtered from the questionnaire, and also personnel from Espresso House.

During phase 2, we planned to communicate with Espresso House personnels andput on a poster near the queuing line, and observe around to count how many people showed interest and scanned the QR code, and how many people actually used the prototype, to see the percentage.

Then ask those who used the prototype if they would like to participate in our evaluations, if so we then send out the usability questionnaire with the follow-up open-ended interview questions.

During phase 3, we planned to send out the same questionnaire as in phase one, to gather the opionions when they’ve used the prototype and how they feel the value invloved in coffee experience changed. We planned to compare the two questionnaire results to see the more reliable difference.

Data analysis - AttrakDiff

We combined qualitative and quantitative data analysis.

Quantitative - Attrakdiff

Normal coffee house experience

(14 participants)

Rated as task oriented. Put value more on pragmatic aspects (PQ 1.29) than on hedonic aspects (HQ 0.31), think coffee experience a more task-oriented activity. Seeing the adjectives on the upper right diagram, the normal coffee experience is defined as human, integrating, conservative and good.

Gamified coffee house experience

(7 participants)

Rated as neutral. Pragmatic aspects drops below ordinary standards (PQ -0.55), hedonic aspects not as strong (HQ 0.05). Hedonic quality stimulation is higher than before (left diagram). Seeing the adjectives with the most difference, the experience is more technical, alienating, innovative and bad.

Quantitative - Affinity diagramming

According to the articile “Using Affinity Diagrams to Evaluate Interactive Proto- types” by our teacher Andrés (Lucero, 2015), we used affinity diagram to analyse the notes gathered during the interviews. Since we were working remotely, we developed our own way to collaborate on online affinity diagram through Miro.

Step 1 - Take the notes

Each group member reviewed 4 participants and aimed to generate approxi- mately one hundred notes per two hours of interview footage (200 notes) to build the wall. We used different colors to differtiate the participants. During this step, we gathered approximately 554 notes.

Step 2 - Making preliminary clusters

During this step each of us looked into other’s notes to prevent the ownership issue. Due to the remote way of conducting affinity diagram, we decided to first create our own clusters with “blue notes”, then combine all the individual clusters together to build the affinity wall.

Step 3 - Walking the wall

Each of us had different ways to cluster, some catagorised all the positive and negative notes, some even had their own “pink notes”, their catagories. And we decided to discard the “positive - negative” scale and use this in the next step.

Step 4 - “Blue notes” and “pink notes”

All the group could discuss together the most recurring ones in order to identity the poignant themes and concepts. Findings have been further labelled, documented and gathered to deter- mine the numerical output of the categories, by groups and by each interview participant. During this step, we firstly made 79 blue notes and 32 pink notes, then further trimmered to 18 blue notes and 9 pink notes.

Affinity diagramming Findings

We had eventually 9 pink notes and 18 blue notes. The blue notes are in the form of “quotes”, which are supported references drawn from the interview notes or interpreted from the conclusions.

Quantitative - User satisfaction questionnaire

Results from this questionnaire indicates and supports findings from the former study also. The questionnaire is rated 1 to 5 (1 being participants strongly disagree with the statements and 5 being that the participants strongly agree). There are some key trends from the eight participants.

Participants may agree on accounts such as (order according to importance):

- They are more likely to order the same coffee

- They would be encouraged by incentive

- The interface is consistent

Learnings

Brief: gaming provides an alternative coffee experience where people can take time and feel comfortable during ordering.

Research Question 1: “What do customers value in a coffee house experience?”

Users enjoy traditions and social interaction.They don’t expect technical aspects and rely on their own taste and routines. They tend to be focused on the activity itself, whilst still enjoying it as well.

Research Question 2: “How does the game affect the experience?”

- A gamified experience could bring value in terms of playing with a friend, opening also some social interactions with the barista and surroundings.

- The game could bring more value by giving users more room to decide and by making choices more simply.

- The majority of the users were indifferent or negative about the value the game could bring, as the recommendations are generated without any control about the outcome nor the process.

- While in fact, introducing randomness is appealing to some participants, however most users demand more control during the process.

- Users need to be incentivised in order for them to be willing to use the platform.

Future Improvement


A list of suggestions are provided to show how a gamified coffee experience could add value and avoid removing value, order due to the number of notes mentioned.

1. Respect people’s coffee tradition #24 notes

Users have their own coffee traditions. Many of them are not expecting to use the phone for gamified experience at a coffee shop. For those who are willing to talk to the barista, interact with friends in the queueing line. Enhancing game experience it needs to address how users can keep their traditions while letting them get interest- ed and create some difference out of the game. By simplifying and optimising the game, making it blended into the environment, customers could be efficient and not distracted from their routines.

2. Give user fair amount of control #23 notes

The game should give users a fair amount of control over the game process (i.e. they can go back to the last choice), and a lot of control over the ingredients going into the coffee. This would enable them to ‘play’ with confidence and enable them to achieve a desired end result (e.g. ingredients, special requirements) and receive more reliable recommendations.

3. People keep opinions open on recommendations #16 notes

Some are willing to try out recommended drinks, some would prefer their usual choice. It is important to keep the recommendations relevant to the context of the user and the end-result of the game. Another important aspect for users’ is their openness to suggestions and the scale of the recommendation; smaller changes, or more subtle recommendations on top of the regular order are preferred.

4. Less randomness #6 and more simplicity #8 notes

Increase options for personal choices (special requirements, extras, etc.) instead of random ingredients. Reduce the rounds from four to three or even less “layers”. Give users a clear path to follow and provide some options to keep them in control (go back, return, change ingredients, etc.). Users demand simplicity: all information given should be relevant to the task.

5. Further develop a game also for non-gamers #9 notes

Make the game to look, feel and function more as a service (exclusive orders, loyalty programme etc.) than a game for users that don’t usually play mobile games. Develop the game functions to be more integrated to the regular ordering process. Users like the aspect of fun, given that the digital service stays the focal point of the experi- ence. Gamification is fine, but a dedicated game on it’s own is too much.

6. Build in financial incentives #8 notes

Some are willing to try out recommended drinks, some would prefer their usual choice. It is important to keep the recommendations relevant to the context of the user and the end-result of the game. Another important aspect for users’ is their openness to suggestions and the scale of the recommendation; smaller changes, or more subtle recommendations on top of the regular order are preferred.
