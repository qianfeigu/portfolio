A Game Designed for
Espresso House

UX Design & Usability Testing

Team project/ Feb. 2020 - Apr. 2020

The project was innitiated by the course “Evaluating Interactions” in Aalto by Associate Professor Andrés Lucero. The goal of the project is to train our skills in finding the probelm from real-life scenario, designing a digital solution, and evaluating the solution.

Problem definition - Field Research

The picture above is the floor plan of Espresso House Kamppi, the place we were looking for pain points through observation. There were two baristas behind the counter in charge of ordering services, customers were waiting in line for two baris- tas to call the next. The menu can only be seen clearly when talking to the barista.

How Might We provide an alternative ordering experience which is less stressful so people can take their time to make decision?

Designing

Mapping

After reclustering all the notes, we found 4 themes:
- to make the information (menu) transparent and accessible
- to improve the efficiency during queueing & paying
- to emotionally support, give more control to customers
- to encourage the spontaneous interactions among customers
Gathering the votes from all team members, we finally chose the third as our main theme, hence our design brief:

Ideating

Storyboarding

We decided to use digital solutions with these features:
- “Coffee based on mood” Recommendation, which could help people decide and for those willing to try new drinks
- “Coffee Passport”, aka. your personal coffee profile that remem- bers your taste and special requirements, with recommendations according your previous purchase

Prototyping

Researching on the existing Espresso House application, it has these pages & features:

- Homepage for all the shortcuts

- Membership page for quick scan of membership card

- Menu page with all available products

- Purchase history page with latest orders

We decided to keep the menu and membership features, add our personalised recommendation feature into the app.

Testing

We found the main problems of the prototype

1. Personalised recommendation Think about the locus of control given to the users, there is a clear separation in people like/hate the feature

2. Interaction media less chances for people to download an app only to order

3. Flaws in the original features no catagory to see in the menu page not sure how to proceed only with a number

Prototype Iteration

Beginning from the feedbacks, we focus on:

1. Personalised recommendation We can make the recommendation feature more fun and engaging, for those who would like to try the feature.

2. Interaction media What can be the more sustainable media type allows people to use while not creating extra hussle? We decided to use HTML webpage on any paper-media like a poster, so the customers can scan the QR code on and access the prototype.

3. Purpose and use cases The new experience can be designed to use on many occassions. We want to focus on creating a soothing queuing experience. 

Instead of combining the Espresso House application, we decided to mainly focus on the recommendation feature itself. So how to make it more fun, at the same time give customers decent amount of control? These are the ideas:
- Coffee Trivia Quiz
- Interactive Video Walk-through,
- Self-service Systems
- Mobile Gaming

Inspired by the games, we found our way: to make the prototype a game, during which the customers could get a coffee recommendation rounds of questions based on luck, and they could also make coffee for friend and receive coffee made by him/her. In this way they can surprise each other and get recommendations.

User scenario

Imagine you arrive to meet your friend at Espresso House. The atmosphere is lively and you join a large queue. There might be ten or twelve people waiting to place their order. Your friend asks you to “play this game” with him/her that he/she discovered from a nearby poster.

Detailed research documentation


Under this section, I included a detailed process of my research in practice and analysis. If you are interested, continue from here!

1. Questionnaire - under 20 participants

We chose the AttrakDiff platform for the questionnaire, with measures of hedonic and pragmatic aspects of experience shown on the picture above.

Phase 1 Expert Interview - 2 participants, former baristas

1. Can you describe how a customer places an order when they arrive?

2. Can you elaborate for me please?

3. What is your role in this process? How do you attend to customers?

4. Which coffee shop aspects are most helpful for customers?

5. Which buyer aspects are most helpful for Coffee shop personnel?

6. If you were a customer, how might you improve the service and why?

7. From the perspective of a coffee shop, how might you improve the service?why?

8. After discussing these, which aspects would you consider to be most valuable or least valuable?

9. Have you any other experiences or information which you feel relevant to our study?

Phase 2 Testing prototype - 8 participants (7 + 1 pilot)

This step is the most challenging step, since we planned to observe the percentage of people would be interested and scan the QR code on the poster. Also from the field research on Week 1, the environment of the coffee shop, the people who are standing in line waiting for ordering, and many other factors would impact on the coffee ordering experience.

To combine the environment, the scenario and the prototype, we made an interactive online pdf with every page linked that the customers would use during the test.

As interviewer and facilitator ourselves, we decided to interview people individually according to the same script, through online video call applica- tions, and take the participants’ screen recordings and voice recording under their concent, for the future data analysing stage.

1. entry-level questions to warm up, know more about personal habits

2. instruct the participants to open the link to the prototype, and read the scenario

3. tell the participants to “think aloud”

4. give some feedback and instructions if needed

Data analysis - AttrakDiff

We combined qualitative and quantitative data analysis.

Quantitative - Attrakdiff

Normal coffee house experience

(14 participants)

Rated as task oriented. Put value more on pragmatic aspects (PQ 1.29) than on hedonic aspects (HQ 0.31), think coffee experience a more task-oriented activity. Seeing the adjectives on the upper right diagram, the normal coffee experience is defined as human, integrating, conservative and good.

Gamified coffee house experience

(7 participants)

Rated as neutral. Pragmatic aspects drops below ordinary standards (PQ -0.55), hedonic aspects not as strong (HQ 0.05). Hedonic quality stimulation is higher than before (left diagram). Seeing the adjectives with the most difference, the experience is more technical, alienating, innovative and bad.

Quantitative - Affinity diagramming

According to the articile “Using Affinity Diagrams to Evaluate Interactive Proto- types” by our teacher Andrés (Lucero, 2015), we used affinity diagram to analyse the notes gathered during the interviews. Since we were working remotely, we developed our own way to collaborate on online affinity diagram through Miro.

Step 1 - Take the notes

Each group member reviewed 4 participants and aimed to generate approxi- mately one hundred notes per two hours of interview footage (200 notes) to build the wall. We used different colors to differtiate the participants. During this step, we gathered approximately 554 notes.

Step 2 - Making preliminary clusters

During this step each of us looked into other’s notes to prevent the ownership issue. Due to the remote way of conducting affinity diagram, we decided to first create our own clusters with “blue notes”, then combine all the individual clusters together to build the affinity wall.

Step 3 - Walking the wall

Each of us had different ways to cluster, some catagorised all the positive and negative notes, some even had their own “pink notes”, their catagories. And we decided to discard the “positive - negative” scale and use this in the next step.

Step 4 - “Blue notes” and “pink notes”

All the group could discuss together the most recurring ones in order to identity the poignant themes and concepts. Findings have been further labelled, documented and gathered to deter- mine the numerical output of the categories, by groups and by each interview participant. During this step, we firstly made 79 blue notes and 32 pink notes, then further trimmered to 18 blue notes and 9 pink notes.

Affinity diagramming Findings

We had eventually 9 pink notes and 18 blue notes. The blue notes are in the form of “quotes”, which are supported references drawn from the interview notes or interpreted from the conclusions.

Quantitative - User satisfaction questionnaire

Results from this questionnaire indicates and supports findings from the former study also. The questionnaire is rated 1 to 5 (1 being participants strongly disagree with the statements and 5 being that the participants strongly agree). There are some key trends from the eight participants.

Participants may agree on accounts such as (order according to importance):

- They are more likely to order the same coffee

- They would be encouraged by incentive

- The interface is consistent

- It is more interesting than the decision making process

- They are less likely to play without incentive

- The game is less likely to interfere with decision making

- They are neutral that the prototype improves the experience
