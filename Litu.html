Litu

Product Design & Product Architecture Design

Team project / Apr. 2020 - May. 2020

The project was initiated by the course “Product Architecture Design” in Aalto, Professor Severi gave lectures on product architecture, product family design, marketing and positioning, circular economy etc. To participate in the Luminord 2020, a competition on luminaires held in Finland, we had another criteria to fulfil sustainability.

Week 2 - Draft user research; Validate initial ideas; Find inspirations; Material research; Ideation

Week 3 - User research analysis; Ideation; Form research; Material research

Week 4 - Sensor & Arduino research; Electrical element research; Clay modelling; Converging ideas

Week 5 - 3D modelling; Business model; Product Architecture; Finalising

We asked ourselves at first: What could a lamp do for us in the extreme conditions of the present, but also in everyday conditions of distance or loneliness?

In our first meeting possible themes emerged

How might these themes manifest in a lighting instrument? Two main ideas to explore


- Connection across distance
- Caring in everyday life
- Support for people in isolation

- Interactivity — human bodies and light
- Connectivity — relationships mediated by/through light

Desktop Research

How can light in our homes help us with the extreme conditions of this moment, and also in everyday conditions of isolation and loneliness that many people face? 

Our research uncovered a possible benefit of time alone — positive self-reflection. Reflective thinking, with the right emotional tenor, can ease loneliness and support personal growth.

User Research

Survey sent to Aalto ARTS students and our various social media networks. Total of 10 valid responses; user age range approx. 20-55 yrs old, mixed gender, multiple nationalities. 

Questions:
- Memories of reflection + connection
- Experience of quarantine
- Ranked response to current lighting concept ideas

Concept

A soft, inviting glow of ambient warm light radiates from a mysterious, organic form. Gently brush your hand across the fronds crowding its surface, and a dazzle of bright light follows your touch like an echo. Like an echo, after a few moments the bright traces fade back to an even glow, waiting quietly, luminously for your next touch.

You are present here, and Litu makes it feel a little less empty.

Component

How it works

Litu’s fronds hold more than 300 warm white LED bulbs, each connected to an individual joystick sensor. 

At rest the LEDs are powered at 60% and provide a total of nearly 1000 lumens. 

Touching a frond triggers its embedded joystick sensor to increase power supply to that bulb to 100%, creating a spike in bulb brightness. Arduino is programmed to continue the 100% brightness for a set time, then dim bulb back to 60%. 

Inviting touch

]Our research uncovered that people in isolation miss touch most of all. Litu invites playful touch and a gentle sense of movement. Its fronds are soft and flexible bio plastic. 

Our design references organic forms that balance chaos and order.


Litu weaves together qualities of light — natural warmth, bright diffusion— that our communities described as most conducive to positive self-reflection. 

Light dancing on water. Light dappling through leaves. Movement in light brings a sense of life. We incorporate movement into our design to bring a sense of liveliness to the room. 

Secure in place

Litu’s illuminated fronds attach to a rigid plastic shell which safely houses the arduino, breadboards and wiring. 

Litu snugly mounts to the wall by screwing the entire lamp unit onto a secure back plate. 

Sustainability

Our design stars an innovative new material: cactus plastic. Developed by Mexican scientist Sandra Pascoe Ortiz, the raw material can be harvested from living cacti, making it plentiful and easily renewable.

“This Oceanworks Guaranteed PP (Polypropylene) Ocean Plastic is sourced from Asia and comes in the form of dark grey pellets. It is guaranteed to have been harvested from an oceanbound location, one of the five types of locations where ocean plastic is collected worldwide, which means that it is material collected from communities with no waste management within 50 km of the shore line. Many small islands of developing countries fall into this category. This is in accordance with standards set by renowned marine debris expert, Jenna Jambeck.”

We use reclaimed plastic ocean waste to create an injection-molded rigid base that holds Litu’s illuminated fronds. The shell is smooth, durable and perfect for neatly and safely containing the electrical components.
